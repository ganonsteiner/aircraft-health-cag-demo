CESSNA MODEL 172N SKYHAWK
PILOT'S OPERATING HANDBOOK — SECTION 7: AIRPLANE AND SYSTEMS DESCRIPTION
1978 Edition (FAA Approved)

Aircraft: N4798E  Serial: 17270798  Engine: Lycoming O-320-H2AD

================================================================================
ENGINE
================================================================================

Model:          Lycoming O-320-H2AD
Type:           Four-cylinder, horizontally opposed, direct-drive, air-cooled,
                fuel-injected (NOTE: H2AD is carbureted via Marvel-Schebler MA-4SPA)
Displacement:   319.8 cubic inches
Bore x Stroke:  5.125" x 3.875"
Horsepower:     160 HP at 2700 RPM (maximum continuous)
Compression:    8.5:1

H2AD Variant Note:
The O-320-H2AD uses barrel-shaped (cylindrical) hydraulic lifters and a matching
wide-lobe camshaft, designed to allow tappet servicing without splitting the
crankcase. In service, this design proved susceptible to cam lobe spalling under
normal operating loads, prompting Lycoming to issue multiple service bulletins and
FAA to issue AD 80-04-03 R2 requiring recurring inspection. Many operators have
since converted to the O-320-D2J configuration. N4798E retains the original H2AD
variant. Compliance with AD 80-04-03 R2 is mandatory and tracked at each oil change.

================================================================================
FUEL SYSTEM
================================================================================

Type:           Gravity-feed, non-pressurized
Fuel Tanks:     Two integral aluminum wing tanks
  Left tank:    21.5 gallons usable (22.5 total)
  Right tank:   21.5 gallons usable (22.5 total)
  Total:        43 gallons usable (45 total)

Flow Path:      Each wing tank drains through fuel caps and vented through NACA
                flush vents on top of each wing. Fuel flows by gravity from the
                selected tank through the selector valve in the cockpit, through
                the gascolator (main fuel strainer/sediment bowl) on the firewall,
                through the electric primer, into the Marvel-Schebler MA-4SPA float-
                type carburetor, and into the intake manifold.

Selector Valve: Four positions: LEFT, BOTH, RIGHT, OFF
  Normal ops:   BOTH (allows cross-feed and balances tanks)
  Best practice: Avoid running on one tank to less than 1/4 remaining;
                 always check both tanks for takeoff.

Gascolator:     Located low on firewall. Contains 60-mesh screen and water trap.
                Should be drained at each preflight to check for water/debris.
                Covered by AD 2000-06-01 (fuel strainer inspection).

Primer:         Engine-driven priming system draws fuel from left tank.

IMPORTANT (AD 2001-23-03): The left forward doorpost contains a map light switch
and a fuel line in close proximity. Inspect the plastic insulator on the map light
switch terminals annually per AD 2001-23-03. A deteriorated insulator can allow
electrical arcing to burn through the adjacent fuel line.

================================================================================
OIL SYSTEM
================================================================================

Type:           Wet sump, pressure-fed
Capacity:       8 quarts maximum (6 quarts minimum for flight)
Oil Pump:       Engine-driven gear-type pump
Pressure Relief: Set at 90 psi
Full-flow Filter: Spin-on filter (Lycoming-approved) — cut open and inspect at
                  every oil change per AD 80-04-03 R2 for metallic particles

Oil Cooler:     Air-cooled, mounted to engine mount structure
  Oil Temp:     Normal range 160–220°F; maximum 245°F

Oil Change Interval: 50 hours or 4 calendar months, whichever occurs first
  (per Lycoming SB 480F and FAA-accepted practice)

Anti-wear Additive: One 6-oz can Lycoming LW-16702 at each oil change required
  per AD 80-04-03 R2 UNLESS using pre-additized oils (AeroShell W 15W-50,
  AeroShell W 100 Plus, Exxon Elite 20W-50) which incorporate equivalent chemistry.

Oil Consumption: Normal: up to 1 quart per 5 hours
                 Investigate if exceeding 1 quart per 3 hours
                 Excessive consumption may indicate worn rings, valve seals,
                 or cam/lifter wear (specific H2AD concern)

================================================================================
ELECTRICAL SYSTEM
================================================================================

System Voltage:  28-volt DC (nominal)
Alternator:      Engine-driven, 60-amp output
Battery:         24-volt lead-acid, 14.5 amp-hour (minimum)
                 Located in left rear of fuselage, accessible from baggage area

Bus Architecture:
  Main Bus:      Powered by alternator and battery in parallel when master switch ON
  Essential Bus: Subset of main bus; avionics master switch controls separately

Circuit Protection: Combination of circuit breakers (push-to-reset) and fuses.
  Most avionics protected by pull-type circuit breakers on instrument panel.

Ammeter:         Shows alternator output current (positive = charging)
  Normal:        Slight positive deflection after engine start and during cruise
  Zero:          Alternator may have failed; run on battery only
  Negative:      Battery discharging; alternator not charging

External Power:  Ground power receptacle on left side of fuselage (AD 77-12-08
                 compliance required — inspection of ground power socket wiring)

================================================================================
VACUUM SYSTEM
================================================================================

Vacuum Pump:    Engine-driven, dry-type (Rapco/Weldon or equivalent)
                NOTE: Dry vacuum pumps have limited service life (~500-800 hours)
                      and can fail without warning. Covered by AD 2006-03-08.

Vacuum Gauge:   In-cockpit indicator, should read 4.5–5.4 in. Hg in cruise
Suction Relief: Calibrated spring-loaded valve on firewall

Gyro Instruments driven by vacuum:
  Attitude Indicator (AI): Erects to vertical within 5 minutes
  Directional Gyro (DG/HI): Must be aligned to compass periodically (15-min max)

NOTE: If vacuum gauge drops below 4.5 in. Hg in flight, gyro instruments become
unreliable. Transition to partial-panel flying using turn coordinator (electric)
and magnetic compass.

================================================================================
PITOT-STATIC SYSTEM
================================================================================

Pitot Tube:     Left wing leading edge (heated pitot tube, electric)
Static Ports:   Two flush-mount ports on left and right fuselage sides (cross-
                connected to cancel yaw errors)
Alternate Static: Knob below instrument panel; opens to cabin static pressure
                  (slightly higher than ambient; expect slight altimeter error)

Instruments Fed:
  Airspeed Indicator (ASI)
  Altimeter
  Vertical Speed Indicator (VSI)

IFR Certification Inspection: Required every 24 calendar months per 14 CFR 91.411.
  Includes encoder altimeter inspection.

================================================================================
LANDING GEAR
================================================================================

Type:           Fixed tricycle gear (non-retractable)

Nose Gear:      Steerable via rudder pedals through 30° each side
                Oleo (air/oil) shock strut — service with MIL-H-5606 hydraulic
                fluid and compressed air (60 psi fully extended with no weight)
                Shimmy damper: Installed on nose gear; replace if shimmy noted

Main Gear:      Tubular steel spring gear; no oleos or shock absorbers
                Main gear tubes occasionally crack — inspect at annual

Tires:
  Nose:         15×6.0–6 (6-ply rating), 26 psi
  Main:         6.00–6 (6-ply rating), 35 psi

Brakes:         Hydraulic single-disc brakes, left and right
                Operated by top portion of rudder pedals (toe brakes)
                Parking brake: Pull knob between seats, press toe brakes, release pedals
                
  Brake Fluid:  MIL-H-5606 red hydraulic fluid
  Master Cyl:   One per pedal, mounted to rudder pedal assembly
  Caliper:      Single-piston sliding caliper type

================================================================================
FLIGHT CONTROLS
================================================================================

Primary Controls: Conventional three-axis (aileron, elevator, rudder)
                  All cable-actuated from dual yokes and rudder pedals

Ailerons:       Differential — down aileron travels less than up aileron to
                reduce adverse yaw. Cable-driven from yoke.
                Aileron balance weights required — see AD 83-17-06.

Elevator:       Cable-driven from yoke (push/pull)
Rudder:         Cable-driven from pedals (push-pull tubes aft)

Trim:           Elevator trim via large wheel on center console; cable-driven
                No aileron or rudder trim installed

Flaps:          Manual "Johnson bar" lever on floor between seats
                Electric flap systems NOT installed on 172N
                Positions: 0°, 10°, 20°, 30° (full)
                Limited to 85 KIAS (Vfe)

Control Cables: 7×7 galvanized aircraft cable; inspect annually for wear,
                fraying, and correct tension. See cable tension table in
                Maintenance Manual Chapter 27.

================================================================================
AVIONICS (AS CURRENTLY INSTALLED IN N4798E)
================================================================================

Comm Radio:     Installed in stack (see avionics panel)
Nav Radio:      VOR/ILS receiver with CDI
Transponder:    Mode C transponder; 24-month inspection per 14 CFR 91.413
                (AD 98-14-03 and 2005-01-19 may apply depending on installed unit)
ELT:            Fixed ELT; battery replacement per 14 CFR 91.207
                Encoder:    Blind encoder for Mode C altitude reporting

================================================================================
SEATS AND BELTS
================================================================================

Seats:          Two front bucket seats, manually adjustable fore-and-aft on
                floor rails using a springloaded locking pin.
                Two rear bench seats (fixed position)

Seat Rails:     Subject to recurring inspection per AD 2011-10-09 (every 100 hr
                or 12 calendar months). Seat slippage on takeoff is a known fatal
                accident cause in Cessna singles.

Seat Belts:     Lap belts: Required for all occupants
                Shoulder Harnesses: Installed in front seats (required for IFR)
                Rear shoulder harnesses: May or may not be installed
                See AD 2004-19-01 (upper shoulder harness adjuster inspection)
